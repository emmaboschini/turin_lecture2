Development cooperation combines the concepts of development and cooperation and can be understood from a political perspective, where politics is closely related to power and operates in both formal and informal contexts. It exists at the intersection of  practices and political processes.
Actors engage in development assistance for multiple reasons. These motivations may be altruistic, economic, political, military, or humanitarian. Development assistance is often used to pursue different objectives such as promoting economic growth, reducing poverty, improving governance, supporting infrastructure, ensuring loan repayment, combating terrorism, spreading specific ideologies, or advancing political and economic influence.
From an institutional perspective, development cooperation policy refers to a set of policies implemented by governments or multilateral institutions aimed at creating the conditions for sustainable economic and social development in other countries. These policies are carried out by governmental organizations or non-governmental organizations at national or international levels.
Development cooperation must be understood within a global context shaped by major challenges such as climate change, conflicts, international migration, and the emergence of new donors with different approaches. Other economic flows, such as foreign direct investment and remittances, often exceed development aid in importance. There is also an ongoing debate about the relevance of development cooperation, including critical perspectives on development models, alternative forms of development, post-development theory, and post-colonial approaches.
Recent global events, particularly the COVID-19 pandemic, have exposed structural vulnerabilities in health systems, environmental management, and socio-economic equality both within and between countries. The crisis highlighted the need for stronger public health systems, resilient supply chains, global partnerships, environmental protection, welfare systems, and poverty reduction. It also demonstrated the importance of international scientific cooperation, global alliances, and community participation.
Institutional transformations have also influenced development cooperation. At the international level, there has been a shift from the Millennium Development Goals to the Post-2015 Agenda and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). At the national level, countries such as Italy have reformed their development cooperation systems, while universities increasingly contribute to development cooperation through research, training, and partnerships.
A key conceptual shift has occurred from “international development” to “global development.” Traditionally, international development focused mainly on improving conditions in poorer countries and addressing inequalities between developed and developing nations. In contrast, global development emphasizes the interconnected nature of globalized capitalism, global public goods such as health and environmental protection, the global challenge of sustainable development, the climate crisis, and inequalities both within and among countries. It also reflects the growing interdependence between the Global North and South.
The international development agenda evolved significantly with the adoption of the Millennium Declaration in 2000 and the subsequent adoption of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development in 2015 by the United Nations. The Sustainable Development Goals establish global objectives for social, economic, and environmental sustainability. They are structured around goals, targets, and indicators that measure progress. These indicators are periodically updated and provide measurable benchmarks to assess development outcomes.
The concept of sustainable development also builds on earlier work such as the Brundtland Commission’s report Our Common Future (1983–1987), which emphasized the importance of balancing economic growth, environmental protection, and social wellbeing.
In the Italian context, development cooperation forms an integral part of foreign policy. Italian legislation, including Law 49 of 1987 and Law 125 of 2014, establishes development cooperation as a means to promote solidarity among peoples, protect human rights, reduce poverty and inequality, support sustainable development, prevent conflicts, and promote peace. Italian policy also emphasizes gender equality, humanitarian aid, and citizen participation in international solidarity.
According to international frameworks, development cooperation has several major tasks. These include supporting developing countries in guaranteeing universal social standards and human rights, promoting economic convergence and reducing global inequalities, and helping countries contribute to international public goods such as vaccines or fair trade systems.
To qualify as development cooperation, activities must meet four criteria. First, they must explicitly support national or international development priorities. Second, they must not be driven by profit, although they may encourage economic activities with positive developmental outcomes. Third, they must intentionally favor developing countries by addressing structural constraints that limit their development opportunities. Fourth, they must be based on cooperative relationships that enhance the ownership and autonomy of developing countries in shaping their own development strategies.
Development cooperation is therefore more than simply transferring financial resources. It includes actions such as reducing trade barriers for developing countries or supporting policy changes that improve development outcomes.
The main purposes of development cooperation include guaranteeing basic social protection standards, promoting convergence in living standards across countries, and supporting the provision of international public goods. Its main characteristics are that it supports development priorities, is not profit-driven, favors developing countries, and is based on cooperative partnerships. Development cooperation can take different forms, including financial or in-kind transfers, capacity building through training and technology cooperation, and policy change initiatives.
Various instruments and mechanisms are used in development cooperation, including budget support programs, solidarity taxes, syndicated loans, and social impact bonds, which link investment returns to measurable social outcomes. International partnerships and alliances, such as the GAVI, the Vaccine Alliance, demonstrate how global cooperation supports public health and development objectives.
It is important to distinguish development cooperation from related concepts. Development cooperation is broader than “aid,” which typically refers only to concessional resource transfers from donor countries. It is also part of international cooperation, although not all forms of international cooperation focus on development. The provision of international public goods, financing for development, and international public finance are broader categories that may include activities not specifically aimed at development or not focused on supporting developing countries.
Overall, development cooperation represents a complex field that combines political, economic, and social dimensions. It seeks to promote sustainable development, reduce global inequalities, strengthen international partnerships, and improve living conditions worldwide through cooperative and non-profit-oriented actions.
